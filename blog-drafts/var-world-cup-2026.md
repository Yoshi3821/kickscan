## The Technology That Divided Football

Love it or hate it, VAR (Video Assistant Referee) has fundamentally altered the way football is played, watched, and — crucially — bet on. Since its full introduction at the 2018 World Cup in Russia, VAR has overturned hundreds of decisions, sparked countless debates, and quietly reshaped the sport's DNA.

Now, with the **2026 FIFA World Cup** set to be the biggest tournament in history — 48 teams, 104 matches, across three countries — VAR's role is more important than ever.

Here's how it changed the game, and what it means for the spectacle ahead.

---

## A Brief History of VAR

VAR was first tested in the Dutch Eredivisie in 2016 and officially adopted by FIFA for the **2018 World Cup in Russia**. That tournament saw **335 incidents reviewed** and **20 decisions changed** — including a dramatic penalty awarded to France in the World Cup Final against Croatia.

By the **2022 World Cup in Qatar**, the system had evolved further. FIFA introduced **semi-automated offside technology (SAOT)**, using 12 dedicated tracking cameras and limb-tracking AI to produce those striking 3D offside graphics in seconds rather than minutes.

The numbers tell the story:

| Tournament | Incidents Reviewed | Decisions Overturned | Avg. Review Time |
|-----------|-------------------|---------------------|-----------------|
| 2018 Russia | 335 | 20 | 80 seconds |
| 2022 Qatar | 200+ | 25 | 25 seconds |
| 2026 (Expected) | Higher volume | Faster | Sub-15 seconds |

## How VAR Changed the Way Teams Play

### 1. The Death of Diving (Mostly)

Before VAR, simulation was a legitimate tactical tool. Players could win penalties, free kicks, and even get opponents sent off with a well-timed tumble. VAR hasn't eliminated diving, but it's made it far riskier.

In the 2022 World Cup, multiple penalty decisions were reversed after VAR reviews showed minimal contact. Players now know that every inch of the pitch is being watched from multiple angles.

**Impact for 2026:** Expect fewer theatrical falls in the box and more genuine attempts to stay on their feet. Teams that relied on "winning" fouls will need to adapt.

### 2. Defending Has Become Surgical

Center-backs can no longer rely on subtle shirt-pulls at corners or sneaky pushes at set pieces. Every dead ball is a VAR minefield. The result? Defending from set pieces has become more about positioning, timing, and athletic ability rather than dark arts.

**Impact for 2026:** Set pieces will remain critical (they accounted for roughly 30% of World Cup goals in 2022), but the nature of defending them has changed permanently.

### 3. Offside Is Now Binary

The old days of "benefit of the doubt goes to the attacker" are gone. Semi-automated offside technology measures to the millimeter. This has led to some controversial "toenail offside" decisions, but it's also removed one of football's most debated gray areas.

**Impact for 2026:** Teams will drill their offside traps with even more precision. Expect more goals disallowed by tiny margins — and the emotional rollercoasters that come with them.

### 4. The Penalty Explosion

VAR has dramatically increased the number of penalties awarded in major tournaments. Incidents that referees previously missed or waved away — a slight clip in the box, a handball from a deflection — are now caught and punished.

- **2014 World Cup (no VAR):** 13 penalties awarded
- **2018 World Cup (first VAR):** 29 penalties awarded
- **2022 World Cup:** 23 penalties awarded

**Impact for 2026:** With 104 matches (up from 64), the total penalty count could be record-breaking. This has massive implications for betting markets — penalty scorers, clean sheets, and over/under goals.

## What VAR Means for World Cup 2026 Specifically

### The Scale Challenge

2026 is unprecedented: **48 teams, 16 groups, 104 matches, 3 host countries.** That's a massive logistical challenge for VAR operations. FIFA will need VAR hubs potentially across multiple time zones, with dozens of trained video officials working in rotation.

The risk? Inconsistency. With more officials reviewing more games, the chances of differing interpretations increase. FIFA is aware of this and has been standardizing training, but it's a genuine concern.

### New Technology Expected

FIFA has hinted at further technological advances for 2026:

- **Improved SAOT** with even faster processing
- **Potential AI-assisted foul detection** (still in testing)
- **Enhanced broadcast integration** — fans in stadiums may see VAR replays faster
- **Connected ball technology** (first used in 2022) with more data points

### The Emotional Factor

In a tournament where underdogs from expanded qualifying will face footballing giants, VAR could be the great equalizer — or the great heartbreaker. A correctly awarded penalty for a team like New Zealand against Brazil could change the course of an entire group.

Conversely, a controversial VAR decision in the knockout rounds, in front of 80,000 fans at MetLife Stadium, could become one of the most talked-about moments in football history.

## What This Means for Bettors

VAR has created both opportunities and challenges for football bettors:

### Opportunities
- **Penalty markets** are more predictable — more penalties means more value in "penalty to be awarded" markets
- **Correct score betting** has shifted — the increased goal threat from penalties makes certain scorelines more likely
- **Card markets** — VAR reviews can lead to retrospective yellow/red cards that wouldn't have been given before

### Challenges
- **Goal disallowances** can swing in-play markets dramatically — a "goal" that gets overturned by VAR can cost bettors who locked in early
- **Match tempo** — VAR reviews create natural breaks that can kill momentum, making in-play betting more volatile
- **Referee tendencies** — Different VAR officials have different thresholds for intervention, adding an extra variable

VAR isn't perfect. It never will be. Football is a game of passion, chaos, and human error — and some argue that's what makes it beautiful. But the technology isn't going away, and 2026 will be its biggest stage yet.

The teams that understand VAR's implications — that train their defending with cameras in mind, that practice staying on their feet, that drill set pieces knowing every contact will be scrutinized — will have an advantage.
